\begin{abstract}
Monitoring student engagement in real time is a long-standing challenge in education technology. We present an end-to-end deep-learning pipeline that extracts frames from classroom video at one frame per second, localizes individuals with YOLOv8-nano, and classifies each crop into one of five facial-reaction classes: Neutral, Confused, Smiling/Amused, Surprised, and Bored/Tired. Two ImageNet-pretrained convolutional architectures---ResNet18 and VGG16---are fine-tuned under identical training protocols that include class-weighted cross-entropy loss, data augmentation, and stratified splitting.

On a held-out test set of 401 manually annotated crops, ResNet18 achieves 90.0\% accuracy (weighted F1 0.900), outperforming VGG16 at 87.3\% (weighted F1 0.874). We supplement classification metrics with Grad-CAM attention maps, t-SNE feature-space embeddings, chronological telemetry dashboards, and per-class confusion matrices. The best model is exported to ONNX, yielding a $2.89\times$ CPU-latency reduction while maintaining numerical parity within $10^{-5}$. Interactive Gradio demos are deployed on Hugging Face Spaces, and a reference AWS architecture illustrates scalable edge-to-cloud deployment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Understanding how students react during lectures is valuable for both instructors seeking real-time feedback and administrators designing data-driven curricula. Traditional approaches rely on manual observation or periodic surveys, neither of which scales to large cohorts or captures the temporal dynamics of attention and affect~\cite{dewan2019engagement}.

Recent advances in computer vision have made automated facial expression recognition (FER) feasible with commodity cameras. Deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained on datasets such as FER2013~\cite{goodfellow2013challenges} and AffectNet~\cite{mollahosseini2019affectnet} demonstrate that machines can approach human-level accuracy on posed and spontaneous expressions. However, classroom video poses additional challenges: subjects are at varying distances, partially occluded, illuminated unevenly, and often display subtle rather than exaggerated expressions.

This work targets \emph{multi-class reaction recognition} from cropped student images extracted from a real university classroom recording. The pipeline is end-to-end: from raw video ingestion through frame extraction, person detection, manual annotation, model training, evaluation, optimization, and deployment. We compare two widely used transfer-learning backbones---ResNet18~\cite{he2016deep} and VGG16~\cite{simonyan2015very}---under identical preprocessing and training protocols, enabling a fair architectural comparison.

Our contributions are summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A documented and reproducible data pipeline covering frame extraction, YOLOv8-nano person detection, manual annotation, and stratified splitting.
  \item Comparative fine-tuning of ResNet18 and VGG16 with inverse-frequency class weighting, oversampling, and data augmentation.
  \item Comprehensive evaluation comprising per-class confusion matrices, training curves, Grad-CAM interpretability analysis, and t-SNE feature-space visualization.
  \item ONNX export and CPU benchmark demonstrating a $2.89\times$ latency reduction.
  \item A reference AWS architecture for edge inference, streaming analytics, and MLOps retraining.
  \item Public Gradio-based demos deployed on Hugging Face Spaces for reproducibility.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Facial Expression Recognition}
Early FER systems relied on hand-crafted descriptors such as Local Binary Patterns and Histogram of Oriented Gradients. Modern approaches use deep CNNs pretrained on large-scale datasets. Goodfellow~et~al.~\cite{goodfellow2013challenges} introduced the FER2013 benchmark, while Mollahosseini~et~al.~\cite{mollahosseini2019affectnet} curated AffectNet with over one million images. In education contexts, engagement and affect have been studied with both RGB and thermal cameras~\cite{dewan2019engagement}, though most prior work focuses on binary engagement (attentive vs.\ inattentive) rather than multi-class reaction taxonomies.

\subsection{Transfer Learning for Small Datasets}
When labeled data are scarce, transfer learning from ImageNet~\cite{deng2009imagenet} provides a powerful initialization. Yosinski~et~al.~\cite{yosinski2014transfer} showed that lower convolutional layers learn generic edge and texture detectors that transfer well across tasks, while upper layers become increasingly task-specific. Fine-tuning strategies range from training only the classification head to unfreezing one or more convolutional stages, with deeper unfreezing generally improving accuracy at the cost of overfitting risk.

\subsection{Person Detection with YOLO}
YOLO~\cite{redmon2016you} introduced single-shot detection, enabling real-time localization. Subsequent versions refined accuracy and efficiency; Ultralytics YOLOv8~\cite{jocher2023yolo} offers a family of models from nano to extra-large. In our pipeline, YOLO decouples \emph{where} people are from \emph{what} they express, allowing the classifier to focus on tightly cropped facial and upper-body regions.

\subsection{Model Optimization and Interpretability}
ONNX~\cite{onnx2019} provides a portable intermediate representation enabling framework-agnostic inference. Graph-level optimizations (operator fusion, constant folding) and specialized runtimes reduce latency, particularly on CPU-only edge devices. For interpretability, Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017grad} produces class-discriminative heatmaps by weighting feature maps with gradient information, letting practitioners verify that the network attends to semantically meaningful regions.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Data Collection and Preprocessing}

A camera placed in a university classroom (with prior student notification) recorded a one-hour lecture session. Raw \texttt{.mp4} video was downsampled to one frame per second using OpenCV's \texttt{VideoCapture} API, yielding approximately 3\,600 candidate frames. YOLOv8-nano~\cite{jocher2023yolo} was run on each frame with a person-class (class~0) confidence threshold of $0.5$. Detected bounding boxes were cropped and saved as individual JPEG files.

Three annotators then manually sorted the resulting crops into five expression folders (Table~\ref{tab:classes}). Ambiguous or heavily occluded crops were discarded. The final labeled dataset was split in a stratified fashion (random seed~42) into 70\% training (1\,399 images), 10\% validation (200), and 20\% test (401), preserving per-class proportions across all splits (Table~\ref{tab:splits}).

\subsection{Model Architectures}

We compare two well-known CNN families, both initialized with ImageNet-1K pretrained weights from \texttt{torchvision}.

\textbf{ResNet18}~\cite{he2016deep} uses residual (skip) connections that ease gradient flow in deeper networks. We freeze all layers except the final residual block (\texttt{layer4}) and replace the fully connected head with a new \texttt{nn.Linear(512, 5)} layer. Unfreezing \texttt{layer4} allows the model to adapt higher-level feature representations to our domain while keeping earlier, more generic features fixed.

\textbf{VGG16}~\cite{simonyan2015very} employs a uniform stack of $3\times3$ convolutions followed by three fully connected layers. We freeze the entire convolutional backbone (\texttt{features}) and replace only the final classifier layer \texttt{classifier[6]} with \texttt{nn.Linear(4096, 5)}. This is a shallower fine-tuning strategy, relying on VGG's pretrained features being sufficiently expressive for our task.

Both models accept $224\times224$ RGB input normalized with ImageNet channel means $[0.485, 0.456, 0.406]$ and standard deviations $[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]$.

\subsection{Training Configuration}

Table~\ref{tab:hparams} summarizes the training hyperparameters, which are shared across both models to ensure a fair comparison.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Training hyperparameters (shared by both models).}
\label{tab:hparams}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Value} \\
\midrule
Optimizer            & Adam \\
Learning rate        & $0.001$ \\
LR scheduler         & ReduceLROnPlateau (patience 3, factor 0.5) \\
Loss                 & CrossEntropyLoss (class-weighted) \\
Class weights        & $w_c = N / (C \cdot n_c)$ \\
Sampler              & WeightedRandomSampler ($2\times$ epoch) \\
Batch size           & 32 \\
Epochs               & 20 \\
Input size           & $224 \times 224$ \\
Random seed          & 42 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The loss function uses inverse-frequency class weights $w_c = N / (C \cdot n_c)$ where $N$ is the total number of training samples, $C{=}5$ is the number of classes, and $n_c$ is the count of class $c$. A \texttt{WeightedRandomSampler} draws training mini-batches with replacement, using twice the dataset size as the nominal epoch length to further mitigate class imbalance.

Data augmentation applied during training includes \texttt{RandomResizedCrop} (scale 0.8--1.0), \texttt{RandomHorizontalFlip} ($p{=}0.5$), \texttt{RandomRotation}($\pm10^\circ$), and mild \texttt{ColorJitter} (brightness and contrast $\pm0.2$, saturation $\pm0.1$). Validation and test sets use only deterministic resize and normalization. A \texttt{ReduceLROnPlateau} scheduler monitors validation accuracy and halves the learning rate after three epochs without improvement. The best model checkpoint by validation accuracy is retained for evaluation.

\subsection{ONNX Export and Benchmarking}

To enable efficient deployment on edge devices, the best ResNet18 checkpoint is exported to ONNX format using \texttt{torch.onnx.export} with opset version~17 and a dynamic batch axis. The exported graph is validated with \texttt{onnx.checker.check\_model}. CPU inference latency is benchmarked by running 100 timed forward passes (after 10 warm-up iterations) on single $224\times224$ crops using both PyTorch and ONNX Runtime's \texttt{CPUExecutionProvider}. Numerical parity is verified by comparing output logits with \texttt{numpy.allclose} at a tolerance of $10^{-5}$.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Training Dynamics}

Fig.~\ref{fig:curves} shows training and validation loss and accuracy over 20 epochs. ResNet18 converges faster, achieving best validation accuracy of 0.92 at epoch~20, compared to VGG16's best of 0.88. ResNet18 training loss drops below 0.02 by epoch~17, while VGG16 plateaus around 0.20, consistent with its shallower fine-tuning (only one linear layer is trainable). Both models exhibit some validation fluctuation, which is expected given the modest validation set size (200 images) and motivates checkpointing by best validation performance rather than using the final epoch.

For misclassified crops, attention often diffuses to shoulders or background, suggesting that the model relies on positional or contextual shortcuts when facial signal is ambiguous.

Table~\ref{tab:onnx} compares PyTorch and ONNX Runtime inference on CPU. \section{Cloud and System Architecture}

To bridge the gap from a research prototype to a scalable deployment, we designed a reference enterprise architecture on AWS (Fig.~\ref{fig:aws}). The system is organized around three functional paths:

\textbf{Edge inference.} An AWS Greengrass-enabled device in each classroom runs the ONNX model on locally captured frames. Only lightweight JSON payloads (timestamps, class probabilities, person counts) are transmitted upstream, minimizing bandwidth and preserving privacy by keeping raw video on-device.

\textbf{Hot path (real-time).} Inference results flow through AWS IoT Core into Kinesis Data Firehose, which streams data into Amazon Timestream---a purpose-built time-series database. Grafana dashboards query Timestream for live classroom reaction distributions. CloudWatch Alarms trigger AWS Lambda functions for automated notifications (e.g., sustained high confusion levels).

\textbf{Cold path (historical).} Firehose simultaneously lands raw JSON in Amazon S3, partitioned by date and classroom. Amazon Athena provides serverless SQL analytics over this archive, enabling university researchers to run ad-hoc queries across semesters without provisioning infrastructure.

\textbf{MLOps retraining.} Amazon SageMaker pulls accumulated training data from S3, retrains classifiers to counteract data and concept drift, and pushes updated ONNX weights back to edge devices via Greengrass over-the-air (OTA) updates. AWS KMS provides customer-managed encryption keys for all data at rest in S3 and Timestream.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

We presented an end-to-end pipeline for multi-class student reaction recognition from classroom video. Under identical preprocessing and training protocols, ResNet18 achieved 90.0\% test accuracy with a weighted F1 of 0.900, outperforming VGG16 (87.3\%, F1 0.874). The advantage is attributable to ResNet18's deeper fine-tuning strategy, which adapts higher-level features to the target domain. ONNX export reduced CPU inference latency by $2.89\times$ without sacrificing numerical fidelity, enabling real-time processing of classroom-scale person counts.

Grad-CAM analysis confirmed that correct predictions attend to facial regions, while t-SNE embeddings revealed interpretable cluster structure aligned with class semantics. The temporal telemetry dashboard demonstrated how per-frame classification can be aggregated into actionable instructor feedback.

Several directions remain for future work. First, the label taxonomy could be extended beyond five classes to include finer-grained states (e.g., focused writing, phone use). Second, temporal models such as LSTMs or Transformers over frame sequences could capture expression dynamics rather than treating each crop independently. Third, attention-based backbones (e.g., Vision Transformers) and self-supervised pretraining on unlabeled classroom footage could improve accuracy on subtle expressions. Finally, any real-world deployment must undergo rigorous privacy review, informed consent processes, and bias auditing across demographic groups.
